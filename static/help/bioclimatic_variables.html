The curves in the chart represent the distribution of cell values within the selected reporting unit for the stated climate model, bioclimatic variable, and time period. Bioclimatic Departure, as defined here, is a measure of the difference between the historical and future curves. Two future models were selected, a Hot/Wet model (CanESM2) and a Hot/Dry model (HadGEM2-ES).

In figure 1 (low biolimatic departure), there is a significant amount of overlap between the historical curves and the future curves. Where this is the case, the historical climate conditions within the reporting unit are expected to exist in the future (though not necessarily in the same locations within the reporting unit).

In figure 2 (very high bioclimatic departure), the future means deviate significantly from the historical means. In fact, there is no overlap between the historical curves and the future curves. This indicates that the historical climate conditions that existed in the selected reporting unit are not expected to exist in the future.

Bioclimatic Departure vs. Climate Change

Bioclimatic Departure is not the same thing as Climate Change. Although all else being equal, lower levels of climate change will contribute to a lower bioclimatic departure value. A bioclimatic departure value of "Very Low" should be interpreted to mean that the historical climate conditions that existed in the past in the selected reporting unit are expected to exist in the future somewhere in the same reporting unit, although not necessarily in the same locations within that reporting unit. For example, within a selected county, the temperatures that historically existed at lower elevations, may still exist in the same county in the future, but only at higher elevations. In general, reporting units with higher topographic diversity and lower levels of climate change will tend to have lower bioclimatic departure values than flat, topographically homogeneous reporting units with high levels of projected climate change.

We used the CalFire-FRAP 30m California statewide land cover dataset to represent vegetation cover within the study area (CalFire-FRAP 2015). These data were produced by the California Department of Forestry and Fire Protection from the "best available data" land cover data and were cross-walked to macro-groups based on the Manual of California Vegetation that was used in the 2015 California State Wildlife Action Plan (California Department of Fish and Wildlife 2015).

We resampled 30m data the map to the approximately 1km (30 arc second) spatial resolution (consistent with the climate data) using a majority filter in Esri's ArcGIS software (ESRI 2014). The data were then converted to the Network Common Data Form (NetCDF; Rew et al. 1997; UNIDATA 2015).

Afterwards we used the macro-group classification to select the nineteen vegetation types occurring the within the study area (Table 2). Of the thirty-one resampled types only nineteen had a distribution greater than 0.2% or were an upland macro-group and thus were used in the climate exposure analysis.
